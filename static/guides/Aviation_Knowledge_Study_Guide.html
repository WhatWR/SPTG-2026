61
CVR in aviation's "black box" stands for what?
Cockpit Voice Recorder
▼
A) Central Voice Recorder
B) Cockpit Voice Recorder
C) Cabin Video Recorder
D) Command Voice Relay
Answer: B) Cockpit Voice Recorder
The CVR records all cockpit audio including crew conversations, radio communications, and background sounds for the last 2 hours. It is bright orange (not black!) and designed to survive crashes and fires.
62
FDR in aviation's "black box" stands for what?
Flight Data Recorder
▼
A) Flight Data Recorder
B) Flight Distance Reader
C) Fuel Data Register
D) Final Descent Recorder
Answer: A) Flight Data Recorder
The FDR records over 88 parameters including altitude, airspeed, heading, vertical acceleration, and control surface positions for the last 25 hours of flight. Together with the CVR, it forms what's known as the "black box."

⚠️ Fun fact: Black boxes are actually bright orange to help rescuers find them!
63
ELT in aviation stands for what?
Emergency Locator Transmitter
▼
A) Engine Location Tracker
B) Emergency Locator Transmitter
C) Electronic Landing Tool
D) Emergency Light Terminal
Answer: B) Emergency Locator Transmitter
An ELT automatically activates upon impact and transmits a distress signal on 406 MHz (and 121.5 MHz as backup) to Cospas-Sarsat satellites, helping Search & Rescue teams locate a downed aircraft.

🛩️ Flight Controls (Q65–70)
65
The primary flight controls are ailerons, elevator, and ___?
Rudder
▼
A) Flaps
B) Spoilers
C) Rudder
D) Slats
Answer: C) Rudder
The three primary flight controls are:
• Ailerons → Roll (longitudinal axis)
• Elevator → Pitch (lateral axis)
• Rudder → Yaw (vertical axis)

Flaps, slats, and spoilers are secondary/auxiliary controls.
66
Which primary control surface controls the roll of an aircraft?
Aileron
▼
A) Rudder
B) Elevator
C) Aileron
D) Flaps
Answer: C) Aileron
Ailerons are located on the outer trailing edge of each wing. When one aileron goes up, the other goes down — one wing rises, the other drops — creating roll around the longitudinal axis (nose to tail).
67
Which primary control surface controls the pitch (nose up/down)?
Elevator
▼
A) Rudder
B) Elevator
C) Aileron
D) Spoiler
Answer: B) Elevator
The elevator is on the horizontal tail (stabilizer). Pulling back on the yoke raises the elevator → nose pitches up. Pushing forward lowers elevator → nose pitches down. Controls movement around the lateral axis.
68
Which primary control surface controls the yaw (side to side)?
Rudder
▼
A) Rudder
B) Elevator
C) Aileron
D) Flaps
Answer: A) Rudder
The rudder is on the vertical tail fin. Pressing right rudder pedal → nose yaws right. Controls movement around the vertical axis. Used mainly for coordination during turns and crosswind landings.
69
The aileron rotates around which axis?
Longitudinal axis
▼
A) Lateral axis
B) Vertical axis
C) Longitudinal axis
D) Diagonal axis
Answer: C) Longitudinal axis
The longitudinal axis runs from nose to tail (like a skewer through the fuselage). Roll around this axis is controlled by ailerons.

Memory: Longitudinal → Lateral roll → Left/Right wing goes up/down
70
The rudder rotates around which axis?
Vertical axis
▼
A) Longitudinal axis
B) Lateral axis
C) Vertical axis
D) Horizontal axis
Answer: C) Vertical axis
The vertical axis runs top to bottom through the aircraft (like a flagpole). Yaw around this axis is controlled by the rudder.
🔧 Wing Devices & Aerodynamics (Q71–73)
71
What is the primary function of slats on an aircraft wing?
Increase lift at low speed
▼
A) Reduce drag at high speed
B) Increase lift during low-speed operations
C) Control the yaw of the aircraft
D) Reduce fuel consumption during cruise
Answer: B) Increase lift during low-speed operations like takeoff and landing
Slats extend from the leading edge (front) of the wing. They increase the wing's camber and chord, raising the maximum coefficient of lift and allowing flight at lower airspeeds. This prevents stall during takeoff and landing.

⚠️ Slats = Leading edge | Flaps = Trailing edge (both increase lift, flaps also increase drag)
72
What is the primary purpose of winglets on an aircraft?
Decrease drag / save fuel
▼
A) Increase thrust
B) Decrease drag and reduce fuel consumption
C) Improve yaw control
D) Increase maximum speed
Answer: B) Decrease drag and reduce fuel consumption
Winglets are upturned wingtip extensions that reduce induced drag caused by vortices at the wingtip. This improves aerodynamic efficiency and reduces fuel burn by 3–5%. Common on A320neo, Boeing 737 MAX (split-tip winglets), and many modern aircraft.
73
In the lift equation L = CL × ½ρv²S, what does ρ represent?
Air density
▼
A) Wing area
B) Velocity
C) Air density
D) Coefficient of lift
Answer: C) Air density
Breaking down the lift equation: L = CL × ½ρv²S
• L = Lift force (Newtons)
• CL = Coefficient of lift (depends on angle of attack & wing shape)
• ρ (rho) = Air density (kg/m³) — decreases with altitude!
• v = True airspeed (m/s)
• S = Wing reference area (m²)

⚠️ This is why aircraft need longer takeoff runs at high-altitude airports — lower ρ = less lift!

▼

76
What is the freezing point of Jet A-1 fuel?
-47°C
▼
A) -40°C
B) -47°C
C) -60°C
D) -20°C
Answer: B) -47°C
Jet A-1 must have a freezing point of -47°C or below (maximum). This is crucial because fuel in wing tanks can cool down to very low temperatures during long-haul high-altitude flights, especially polar routes.

Jet A (USA): -40°C | Jet A-1 (international): -47°C
🌤️ Atmosphere (Q77–79)
77
In which layer of the atmosphere do commercial aircraft typically fly?
Stratosphere (cruise) / Troposphere (lower)
▼
A) Troposphere
B) Stratosphere
C) Mesosphere
D) Thermosphere
Answer: B) Stratosphere
Commercial aircraft cruise at 35,000–42,000 ft (approximately 10–13 km), which puts them in the lower stratosphere (just above the tropopause). This layer has stable, smooth air with low turbulence — ideal for fuel-efficient cruise.

⚠️ Note: Takeoff and landing occur in the troposphere. Cruise altitude overlaps the tropopause into the lower stratosphere.
78
The troposphere extends from the surface to approximately what altitude?
0–12 to 18 km
▼
A) 0-5 km
B) 0-12 to 18 km
C) 18-50 km
D) 50-80 km
Answer: B) 0–12 to 18 km
The troposphere thickness varies with latitude: ~8 km at poles, ~18 km at the equator (average ~12 km). All weather occurs here. Temperature decreases with altitude at a lapse rate of ~2°C per 1,000 ft (standard).
79
The ozone layer is located in which layer of the atmosphere?
Stratosphere
▼
A) Troposphere
B) Stratosphere
C) Mesosphere
D) Thermosphere
Answer: B) Stratosphere
The ozone (O₃) layer is concentrated between 15–35 km altitude in the stratosphere. It absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV-B and UV-C) radiation from the sun, protecting life on Earth.

💡 IATA vs ICAO — Key Difference

✈️ IATA Codes

  • Airlines: 2 letters (TG, SQ, CX)
  • Airports: 3 letters (BKK, SIN, HKG)
  • Used on tickets, baggage tags, booking systems
  • Managed by IATA (Montreal-based)

📡 ICAO Codes

  • Airlines: 3 letters (THA, SIA, CPA)
  • Airports: 4 letters (VTBS, WSSS, VHHH)
  • Used in ATC, flight plans, official documents
  • VT = Thailand prefix for all Thai airports

🛩️ Aircraft Axes & Control Surfaces

Red = Longitudinal axis (Roll/Aileron) | Green = Lateral axis (Pitch/Elevator) | Yellow = Vertical axis (Yaw/Rudder)

🔄 Roll — Aileron

Axis: Longitudinal (nose→tail)
Control: Aileron (outer trailing edge of wings)
Action: One up, one down — tips wings left/right

↕️ Pitch — Elevator

Axis: Lateral (wingtip→wingtip)
Control: Elevator (horizontal tail)
Action: Nose up/down

↔️ Yaw — Rudder

Axis: Vertical (top→bottom)
Control: Rudder (vertical tail fin)
Action: Nose swings left/right

🔧 Secondary Controls & Devices

🟢 Slats (Leading Edge)

Extend from front of wing. Increase lift at low speeds (takeoff & landing). Prevent stall by allowing higher angle of attack.

🟡 Flaps (Trailing Edge)

Extend from rear of wing. Increase both lift AND drag. Used during takeoff (less) and landing (more).

⬛ Spoilers

Rise from upper wing surface. Destroy lift and increase drag. Used for descent, braking after landing (ground spoilers), and roll assist.

🔺 Winglets

Upturned wingtip extensions. Reduce induced drag from wingtip vortices. Save 3–5% fuel. Now standard on most modern jets.

📦 Black Box & Safety Equipment

🟠 CVR — Cockpit Voice Recorder

Records last 2 hours of cockpit audio. Microphones capture all crew speech, radio, and background noise. Bright orange color.

🟠 FDR — Flight Data Recorder

Records 25+ hours of flight parameters (88+ items): altitude, speed, heading, control positions, engine data. Also orange.

📡 ELT — Emergency Locator Transmitter

Activates on impact. Transmits on 406 MHz to Cospas-Sarsat satellites. Helps SAR teams find crashed aircraft.

🌤️ Atmospheric Layers

✈️ Commercial aircraft cruise in the lower stratosphere (~35,000–42,000 ft)

  • ρ = Air density (kg/m³)
  • v = Velocity/airspeed (m/s)
