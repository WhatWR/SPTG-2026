Commercial Jet Aircraft — Top-down & Side View (Annotated)

1Fuselage
Main body of the aircraft
🔵 Fuselage

The central structural body of the aircraft. Everything else attaches to it. Contains:
• Cockpit — flight deck for pilots
• Passenger Cabin — main cabin
• Cargo Hold — baggage & freight
• Fuel, hydraulic, and electrical systems run through it

Materials: Aluminum alloy (traditional) or Carbon Fiber Reinforced Polymer / CFRP (modern — e.g., Boeing 787)
2Main Wing
Generates lift to fly
🔵 Main Wing

The wing generates Lift by using Bernoulli's principle — air moves faster over the curved upper surface, creating lower pressure. This pressure difference pulls the wing upward.

⚠️ Lift Equation: L = CL × ½ρv²S

Wing tanks also store jet fuel. Typical commercial aircraft has a swept wing design to reduce drag at high subsonic speeds.
3Aileron
Controls ROLL
🟡 Aileron

Located on the outer trailing edge of each wing. They work in opposite directions — one goes up while the other goes down, tilting (rolling) the aircraft.

Controls ROLL
Axis Longitudinal Axis (nose to tail)

⚠️ Exam key: Aileron = Roll = Longitudinal Axis
4Flap
Increases Lift + Drag
🔴 Flap

Located on the inner trailing edge of each wing. When extended, flaps:
• Increase Lift (raise the coefficient of lift)
• Increase Drag (slow the aircraft down)
• Allow flight at lower airspeeds without stalling

Used during: Takeoff (partial) and Landing (full)

⚠️ Flap = Trailing Edge | Slat = Leading Edge
5Slat
Increases low-speed Lift
🟢 Slat (Leading Edge Slat)

Extends from the leading edge (front) of the wing. When extended, slats:
• Increase wing camber and chord
• Raise maximum Coefficient of Lift (CL max)
• Prevent stall by allowing a higher angle of attack

Used during: Takeoff and Landing

⚠️ Primary function: Increase lift at low-speed operations
6Spoiler
Destroys Lift / Air Brake
🟣 Spoiler

Panels that rise from the upper wing surface. Three roles:
• Ground Spoiler — deployed fully after touchdown, destroys all remaining lift so the full aircraft weight loads the wheels for maximum braking
• Flight Spoiler — assists ailerons for roll control
• Speed Brake — deployed during descent to reduce speed without changing thrust
7Engine
Generates Thrust
🔵 Turbofan Jet Engine

How it works: Suck → Squeeze → Bang → Blow
• Fan — draws air in, most bypasses the core (bypass air = most of thrust)
• Compressor — squeezes air to high pressure
• Combustion Chamber — fuel burns, gases expand
• Turbine — extracts energy to spin compressor + fan
• Nozzle — expels hot gas backward → thrust forward

⚠️ Thai Airways A321neo engine: CFM LEAP-1A
8Winglet
Reduces induced drag
🔵 Winglet (Sharklet on Airbus)

Upturned wingtip extensions that reduce induced drag caused by wingtip vortices (spiraling air where high pressure under the wing meets low pressure above).

Benefits:
• Reduces fuel burn by 3–5%
• Does NOT increase thrust
• Does NOT control yaw

Types: Sharklet (A320neo), Split-tip (737 MAX), Blended Winglet (737 NG)
9Vertical Stabilizer
Directional stability
🔴 Vertical Stabilizer (Fin)

The upright tail fin. Provides directional stability — keeps the nose from yawing (swinging) left and right on its own.

Without a vertical stabilizer, the aircraft would weathervane uncontrollably.

The Rudder is hinged to the trailing edge of the vertical stabilizer.
10Rudder
Controls YAW
🔴 Rudder

Hinged to the vertical stabilizer (tail fin). Controlled by rudder pedals at the pilot's feet.

Controls YAW (nose swings left / right)
Axis Vertical Axis (top to bottom)

Uses:
• Crosswind landing corrections
• Coordinating turns with ailerons
• Engine-out asymmetric thrust compensation

⚠️ Exam key: Rudder = Yaw = Vertical Axis
11Elevator
Controls PITCH
🟢 Elevator

Hinged to the horizontal stabilizer (horizontal tail). Controlled by pulling/pushing the yoke or sidestick.

Controls PITCH (nose up / down)
Axis Lateral Axis (wingtip to wingtip)

Pull yoke back → Elevator up → Nose pitches UP (climb)
Push yoke forward → Elevator down → Nose pitches DOWN (descend)

⚠️ Exam key: Elevator = Pitch = Lateral Axis
12Landing Gear
Supports weight on ground
⚙️ Landing Gear

Tricycle configuration (most common in commercial jets):
• Nose Gear — under the nose, steerable for taxiing
• Main Gear — under the wings/fuselage, absorbs landing loads

After takeoff, gear is retracted (raised) to reduce aerodynamic drag.
Systems: Hydraulic extension/retraction, anti-skid brakes, and a gear warning horn.
13Cockpit (Flight Deck)
Pilot control center
🔵 Cockpit / Flight Deck

Key instruments and controls:
• PFD (Primary Flight Display) — speed, altitude, attitude, heading
• ND (Navigation Display) — map, route, weather
• FCU/MCP — autopilot control panel
• Overhead Panel — all aircraft system switches
• Thrust Levers — engine power (throttle)
• Yoke or Sidestick — controls Roll (aileron) and Pitch (elevator)

🔥 Turbofan Jet Engine

Turbofan Engine — Cross-section (Suck · Squeeze · Bang · Blow)

① Fan

Large front blades draw air in. Most air bypasses the core and is accelerated directly — this bypass air produces the majority of thrust in high-bypass turbofans. Also quieter and more fuel-efficient.

② Compressor

Multiple stages of rotating blades compress air to 30–40× atmospheric pressure. Split into LPC (Low Pressure) and HPC (High Pressure) sections. Compresses air before combustion.

③ Combustion Chamber

Fuel (Jet A-1) is injected and ignited with compressed air. Temperatures reach 1,600–2,000°C. Hot expanding gases rush into the turbine section at high velocity.

④ Turbine

Extracts energy from hot exhaust gases to drive the compressor and fan via a central shaft. Consists of HPT (High Pressure Turbine) and LPT (Low Pressure Turbine).

⑤ Nozzle (Exhaust)

Accelerates remaining exhaust gases rearward. By Newton's 3rd Law (action–reaction), this generates thrust pushing the aircraft forward.

✈️ Thai Airways A321neo Engine

Engine: CFM LEAP-1A
Made by CFM International (GE + Safran)
~15–20% more fuel efficient than the CFM56 (CEO)
High bypass ratio = quieter + cleaner emissions

⚙️ Key Aircraft Systems

🟠 CVR — Cockpit Voice Recorder

Part of the "black box" (actually bright orange).

  • Records last 2 hours of cockpit audio
  • Captures crew speech, radio calls, background sounds (alarms, switches)
  • Microphone in the cockpit ceiling area
  • Survives: fire (1,100°C/30 min), impact (3,400 G), water (6,000 m depth)

🟠 FDR — Flight Data Recorder

Part of the "black box" (also bright orange).

  • Records 88+ parameters for the last 25 hours
  • Parameters include: altitude, airspeed, heading, vertical acceleration, control surface positions, engine data
  • Same survival specs as CVR
  • Both units emit an Underwater Locator Beacon (ULB) for 30 days after immersion

📡 ELT — Emergency Locator Transmitter

  • Automatically activates on impact
  • Transmits on 406 MHz to Cospas-Sarsat satellites
  • Also transmits on 121.5 MHz (guard frequency — backup)
  • Helps SAR (Search & Rescue) teams pinpoint a crash site
  • Required on all commercial aircraft

🛡️ Hydraulic System

  • High-pressure fluid (typically ~3,000 psi) powers heavy moving parts
  • Moves: Flight control surfaces, landing gear, flaps & slats, wheel brakes, thrust reversers
  • A320/A321 has 3 independent hydraulic systems: Green, Blue, Yellow (redundancy)

⚡ Electrical System

  • IDG (Integrated Drive Generator) — engine-driven generator, primary source
  • APU (Auxiliary Power Unit) — small tail-mounted engine, used on ground and as backup
  • External Power — Ground Power Unit (GPU) at the gate
  • Battery — emergency backup
  • RAT (Ram Air Turbine) — deploys in total electrical failure to power essential systems

🌡️ Pressurization System

  • At 35,000 ft, outside air is too thin to breathe
  • Bleed air from the compressor is used to pressurize the cabin
  • Cabin altitude maintained at equivalent of 6,000–8,000 ft
  • B787 exception: uses electric compressors → higher humidity → more comfortable
  • Pressure differential across the fuselage skin is constantly managed

🌤️ Layers of the Atmosphere

📐 The Lift Equation — L = CL × ½ρv²S

L = CL × ½ρv²S
SymbolFull NameWhat it means
LLiftUpward force produced by the wing (Newtons)
CLCoefficient of LiftDepends on angle of attack and wing shape — pilot controls this
ρ (rho)Air DensityDecreases with altitude — thinner air = less lift!
vAirspeed (velocity)Faster speed = much more lift (squared effect)
SWing Reference AreaLarger wings = more lift

⚠️ At high altitude airports (e.g., Chiang Mai, Kathmandu), ρ is lower → longer runway needed for the same lift.
The v² term means doubling speed gives 4× more lift — aerodynamics is very sensitive to speed changes.
